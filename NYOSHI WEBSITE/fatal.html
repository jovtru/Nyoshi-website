Facial Recognition Technology

Author and Overview

Author: Jorvan Morgan, Technical Writer and Editor

What is Facial Recognition?

Facial recognition is a category of biometric software that maps an individual's facial features to confirm their identity. Facial recognition is commonly used in security systems to identify specific individuals or users. One of the most common uses of facial recognition is for unlocking smartphones. The technology is also used for law enforcement, video surveillance, and passenger screening.

Facial recognition maps a person's facial features mathematically and stores the data as a faceprint. The software uses deep learning algorithms to compare a separate video or digital image to a database of stored faceprints to verify an individual's identity.

Facial recognition is an important biometric for security and identity verification due to its contactless process. However, the accuracy of facial recognition systems is lower than that of other biometric technologies, such as fingerprint, palm, or iris recognition.

How a Facial Recognition Application Works

Facial recognition works using the following four steps:

  • Detection: Finding a face in a photo, video, or real-time video using computer vision, which uses AI to identify data from inputs like videos or images.
  • Analysis: Identifying approximately 80 nodal points on a human face. Nodal points are used to measure variables like the length or width of the nose, depth of eye sockets, and shape of cheekbones.
  • Faceprint Storage: Storing the captured and analyzed nodal points in a database as a faceprint, which represents the data as a string of numbers or a mathematical formula.
  • Recognition: Comparing the faceprint against a database containing data captured from faces in images or videos.

Even though the facial recognition system only uses 80 nodal points, it can quickly and accurately identify target individuals when conditions are favorable. However, if the face is partially obscured or in profile, the software may be less reliable. The National Institute of Standards and Technology reports that false positives in facial recognition systems have been halved every two years since 1993.

Examples of Facial Recognition

High-quality cameras in mobile devices have made facial recognition viable for both authentication and identification. For instance, Apple’s iPhone X and later models use Face ID technology to unlock phones using a faceprint captured by the camera. The software captures and compares over 30,000 variables to prevent spoofing with photos or masks and can be used for authentication with Apple Pay and other services.

Facebook employs facial recognition to tag people in photos, storing mapping information for future identification. Smart advertisements in airports use facial recognition to target ads based on a passerby’s demographic attributes.

Other examples include Amazon, MasterCard, and Alibaba, which have implemented facial recognition for payment methods, and the Google Arts & Culture app, which identifies museum doppelgangers by matching faces in portraits.

Developers can use services like Amazon Rekognition or Google Cloud Vision API to integrate facial recognition into applications, utilizing machine learning for face detection and analysis in various fields including entertainment and marketing.

Uses of Facial Recognition Technology

  • Mobile Phone Manufacturers: Companies like Apple use facial recognition for security, requiring users to unlock phones using their face.
  • Airports: Facial recognition is used for screening by matching passenger images to physical scans of IDs or passports.
  • Law Enforcement Agencies: These agencies compare mugshots to databases using facial recognition technology.
  • Social Media: Facebook tags individuals in photos using facial recognition.
  • Business Security: Facial recognition provides secure building access for employees.
  • Marketing: Marketers use facial recognition to target specific demographics based on age, gender, and ethnicity.
  • Healthcare: Some healthcare providers use facial recognition to access patient records and detect diseases.
  • Banking: Apps like Bank of America's CashPro use facial recognition for user authentication.

Benefits

  • Increased authentication without physical contact, unlike fingerprint scanners which may be affected by dirt.
  • Enhanced security compared to easily guessed passwords and safer in public settings where passcodes might be observed.
  • Easy integration with existing security features on devices with front-facing cameras.
  • Improved accuracy due to advancements in machine learning, mapping processes, and processing speeds.
  • Automation in airports where biometric passports allow for quicker check-ins and bypassing long lines.

Security and Privacy Concerns

Facial recognition systems can capture and store data without the individual's knowledge, raising concerns about data security and privacy. There is potential for misuse of facial recognition data by hackers, unauthorized data sales, or government surveillance. Privacy issues include the potential for false positives and racial biases in poorly trained systems.

Currently, there is no federal law in the U.S. specifically protecting biometric data, though some state laws like the Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act do regulate biometric data collection and usage. The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the EU also addresses biometric data protection.

Some facial recognition software includes a confidence score to indicate the accuracy of face comparisons, with higher scores indicating greater likelihood of a match.

While facial recognition offers convenience and security, it also presents potential privacy challenges. Understanding these issues is important when evaluating the use of facial recognition by third-party services and government entities.
